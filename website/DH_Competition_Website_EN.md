# Page 2: About the Competition

## 2.1 Competition Overview

The "2026 PolyU AI × Digital Humanities Awards" is a flagship initiative organised by the Faculty of Humanities at The Hong Kong Polytechnic University, open to all full-time undergraduate students of the Faculty. The competition centres on the intersection of artificial intelligence and classical studies, encouraging students to engage deeply with Chinese classical texts, canonical thought, history, culture, translation, and language analysis.

## 2.2 Theme: AI & Classics

Under the theme "AI & Classics," participants are challenged to bridge ancient knowledge with emerging technologies. "Classics" is understood broadly to include classical texts, canonical thought, premodern history and culture, rare-book images, translation of classics, and related forms of knowledge communication. Entries may focus on Chinese classical texts and culture, but may also extend to classical language, reception history, interpretation of canonical texts, or cross-cultural translation, provided that the connection to "classics" or "classical learning" is clearly articulated.

## 2.3 Project Directions

Entries are invited across the following directions. This list is illustrative, not exhaustive.

| Direction | Description |
|---|---|
| **GenAI for Classical Texts** | Use generative AI to support guided reading, comparative translation, annotation design, or interactive Q&A |
| **NLP & Data Visualisation** | Apply NLP, NER, knowledge graphs, GIS and data visualisation to analyse classical texts or historical materials |
| **AI Agent Workflows** | Design automated workflows connecting source extraction → data organisation → content analysis → digital presentation |
| **AI-Enhanced DH Tools** | Integrate tools such as CBDB, MARKUS, DocuSky for text annotation, entity extraction, and visual analysis |
| **AI Teaching Tools** | Design AI tutors, chatbots or digital learning platforms centred on classical content |
| **Datasets & Models** | Build datasets, models or retrieval tools related to classical texts, historical figures, place names, or images |
| **AR/VR & Multimodal** | Use AR/VR, spatial computing or multimodal storytelling to reimagine classical learning or cultural communication |

# Page 4: Submission Requirements

## 4.1 Competition Streams

The competition adopts a three-stream structure. Participants must select a stream upon registration. Entries will be assessed in accordance with the selected stream and the actual nature of the project. If a project spans two streams, participants should explain its principal evaluative positioning in the project abstract.

| Stream | Focus | Typical Entry Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Stream A** | Use of GenAI Tools | Guided reading resources, comparative translation exercises, classroom activities, interactive Q&A, instructional frameworks or learning resources for classics |
| **Stream B** | Development of Tailored AI Tools, AI Agents, or AI Agent Workflows | AI tutors, text-analysis tools, data-extraction tools, knowledge graphs, datasets, retrieval systems, or prototype AI agent workflows |
| **Stream C** | Embodied, Spatial and Multimodal AI for Classics | AR/VR, mapping, image recognition, multimodal generation, digital exhibitions or spatial storytelling to reimagine classical learning and cultural communication |

## 4.2 Accepted Forms of Submission

All entries must clearly address the theme of "AI & Classics" and explain their educational value, research value, or public communication value.

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Research-Oriented Work** | AI-assisted analysis of classical texts, organisation of historical sources, extraction of names, places or concepts, comparative translation study, or pedagogical inquiry |
| **Tool-Based Work** | Website prototypes, AI chatbots, AI tutors, data-visualisation dashboards, retrieval systems, datasets or models |
| **Teaching-Oriented Work** | Curriculum units, interactive learning materials, learning tasks, AI-assisted classics-reading plans or assessment tools |
| **Exhibition-Oriented Work** | Short videos, digital exhibitions, story maps, AR/VR experiences, multimodal narratives, image-classification projects or interactive text-image prototypes |
| **AI Agent Workflow Work** | Projects centred on AI agents or multi-agent collaborative workflows demonstrating a full process from source extraction, data cleaning and annotation, knowledge extraction, analysis and reasoning, to visualisation, story maps, digital exhibitions or instructional outputs. Entries may include workflow diagrams, prompt design, tool orchestration, data-flow design, demonstrable platforms or functional prototypes |

## 4.3 Submission Requirements

### (i) First-Round Submission

All first-round submissions must include the following:

| Item | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Online Application & Abstract** | A completed online application form and project abstract |
| **Introduction Video or Slide Deck** | A project introduction video of no more than 3 minutes (MP4), or a slide deck of no more than 15 pages (PDF/PPT), and a workflow diagram where applicable |
| **Supporting Materials** | Prototypes, websites, code, datasets, models, interactive pages, GitHub repositories or public links, where applicable |
| **AI Use Statement** | A statement specifying the generative AI tools used, the purposes for which they were used, and the scope of human revision, verification and judgement |
| **Source Acknowledgement** | Any use of texts, historical sources, translations, images, maps, datasets or third-party models must be accompanied by source acknowledgement or licensing information |

### (ii) Final Submission after Shortlisting

Shortlisted teams will be notified in early January 2027 and must additionally prepare the following:

| Item | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Revised Materials** | A revised final version of the introduction video and presentation materials |
| **Demonstrable Version** | Tool-, website- or system-based entries must provide a demonstrable version, a recorded demonstration or a testing link |
| **Explanatory Note** | Research-oriented or teaching-oriented entries must provide a 1,000–1,500 word explanatory note outlining the research problem, methodology, AI applications, scholarly basis and limitations |
| **Final Presentation** | Shortlisted teams must attend the final presentation session and, where relevant, conduct a live or online demonstration |

# Page 6: Judging

## 6.1 Judging Criteria

Entries will be assessed across five criteria, combining directions commonly adopted in international AI-in-education competitions with the digital humanities evaluation framework. For entries in Stream B or Stream C with stronger prototype elements, the panel may give greater emphasis to feasibility and functionality. For research-oriented or teaching-oriented submissions, scholarly accuracy and educational design will carry greater weight.

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem Definition and Relevance to Classics / Humanities** | 20% | Whether the project clearly identifies a problem in classical texts, classical cultures, or premodern history/language, and explains its genuine educational or research relevance |
| **Feasibility, Functionality and Technical Realisation** | 20% | Whether the work is workable and technically sound; for tool-based prototypes, whether core functions are demonstrated effectively |
| **Innovation and Creativity** | 20% | Whether the combination of AI and humanities content is original and goes beyond routine or demonstrative use |
| **Scholarly Quality and Research Rigour** | 25% | Whether texts, sources, translations, concepts and references are handled accurately and whether the project is methodologically grounded and well evidenced |
| **Social Impact, Sustainability and Responsible AI** | 15% | Whether the project addresses ethics, copyright, bias, sustainability, scalability and educational equity |

## 6.2 Assessment Procedure

The assessment is conducted in three stages:

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1 — Preliminary Review** | All first-round entries will be assessed by the panel according to the published judging criteria based on written and media materials submitted |
| **Stage 2 — Shortlist and Revision** | Shortlisted teams will be announced and will revise their projects in light of panel feedback |
| **Stage 3 — Final Presentation and Demonstration** | Shortlisted teams will present their work and demonstrate it where applicable to a multidisciplinary judging panel |

## 6.3 Panel Composition

Each shortlisted entry will normally be reviewed by no fewer than three judges. The judging panel will where possible comprise members with disciplinary backgrounds spanning history, language, literature, translation, language sciences, digital humanities and AI applications. External experts may be invited to strengthen fairness and interdisciplinary judgement.

# Page 9: Rules & Contact / FAQ

## 9.1 AI Use and Academic Integrity

All entries must be the original work of the participants. The following requirements apply to all submissions:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Originality** | Entries must not plagiarise, misappropriate or re-submit work that has already won awards in other competitions |
| **AI Disclosure** | Where generative AI is used, participants must disclose the tools used, the prompting approach, the scope of generated content and the extent of human revision |
| **Undisclosed AI Content** | The Organising Committee may disqualify entries or revoke awards in cases of undisclosed AI-generated content, fabricated sources, falsified citations or serious academic misconduct |
| **Copyright and Ethics** | Participants must comply with copyright, privacy, data protection and research ethics requirements; the use of third-party texts, images, maps, datasets or models must be properly licensed or fairly cited |
| **Historical and Cultural Materials** | Projects involving historical figures, geospatial data, rare-book images or cultural heritage materials should attend carefully to accuracy, context and potential bias, and are encouraged to reflect critically on the limitations of AI |

## 9.2 Intellectual Property and Public Display

## 9.3 Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1: Who is eligible to enter?**
Full-time undergraduate students enrolled in the Faculty of Humanities at PolyU, across the four eligible programmes. Please refer to the Eligibility & Teams page for the full list.

**Q2: Can I participate individually?**
Yes. Entries may be submitted individually or in teams of 2 to 4 members. Each student may join only one team as an official member.

**Q3: Do I need programming experience?**
No advanced programming experience is required. Participants may begin with Stream A, which focuses on the effective use of existing GenAI tools. Training workshops will also be provided throughout the competition period to support participants at all levels.

**Q4: Can my project span two streams?**
Yes, but participants must explain the project's principal evaluative positioning in the project abstract at the time of submission.

**Q5: What must be included in the AI use statement?**
The statement must specify the generative AI tools used, the purposes for which they were used, the scope of generated content, and the extent of human revision, verification and judgement.

**Q6: Will my intellectual property be retained?**
Yes. Copyright and intellectual property in submitted work shall, in principle, remain with the participants. The Organising Committee may display the work publicly with due attribution.
